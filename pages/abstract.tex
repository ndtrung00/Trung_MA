\chapter{\abstractname}

Contract Lifecycle Management platforms increasingly rely on large language
models (LLMs) to extract clauses from commercial contracts. Vendors and recent
academic systems claim that splitting the task across several specialised agents
outperforms a single well-prompted model, but no published study tests this
claim head-to-head across modern model families on a shared benchmark.

This thesis compares five extraction setups: zero-shot, chain-of-thought, a
classify-then-extract pipeline, a router that dispatches to three domain
specialists, and a single agent that consolidates the specialist prompts.
Each setup is run with eleven frontier models from Anthropic, Google, and
OpenAI on the 102-contract test split of the Contract Understanding Atticus
Dataset (CUAD), producing 163{,}644 paired predictions compared with
established statistical tests.

No multi-agent setup consistently beats the strongest single-agent baseline.
Overall $F_2$ scores fall within roughly one percentage point of one another
(0.790--0.801), and each provider peaks under a different configuration.
Two findings explain the flat result. The pipeline's first-stage classifier
silently rejects valid clauses on the Gemini models, where it accounts for
nearly all false negatives. The LLM router, in turn, almost always
picks the same specialist that a static category-to-specialist lookup would
have picked, and the single agent given all specialist instructions at once
matches the routed version's $F_2$. The orchestration layer therefore adds
runtime and cost without changing what the model does. Multi-agent setups also cost 23--67\% more per
contract without a matching accuracy gain. Their one durable advantage is
verbatim grounding: every predicted clause is cited from the contract,
compared with about half under zero-shot prompting.

\medskip
\noindent
For legal teams and the vendors building their tools, one well-prompted model
usually reviews contracts as accurately as a more elaborate multi-agent system.
The extra agents earn their cost mainly when a regulator or client needs to
see exactly where each clause came from.
