\chapter{\abstractname}

Contract Lifecycle Management platforms increasingly use large language models
(LLMs) to extract clauses from commercial contracts, and multi-agent architectures
are claimed to outperform single-prompt deployments. Existing legal multi-agent
systems each evaluate a single architecture on a single benchmark, and no published
work ablates multi-agent patterns against single-agent baselines on contract clause
extraction across frontier-model families.

This thesis evaluates five configurations: zero-shot, chain-of-thought,
classify-then-extract pipeline, routed domain specialists, and a consolidated
single-agent ablation that combines all specialist prompts. The configurations
are evaluated across eleven frontier models from three providers (Anthropic,
Google, OpenAI) on the 102-contract test split of the Contract Understanding
Atticus Dataset (CUAD), yielding 163{,}644 paired predictions analysed with
McNemar exact tests, paired bootstrap confidence intervals, and
Benjamini--Hochberg correction.

No multi-agent configuration consistently beats the strongest single-agent
baseline: pooled $F_2$ spans a $0.011$-point band ($0.790$--$0.801$), and each
provider peaks under a different configuration (Anthropic under chain-of-thought,
Google under zero-shot, OpenAI under classify-then-extract). Two mechanisms
explain the null result. The pipeline's classifier silently overrejects on
Gemini~2.5 Flash and Pro, where ${\geq}97.8\%$ of false negatives originate at
the gate. The LLM router agrees with a deterministic category-to-specialist
mapping on $99.91\%$ of requests, and the consolidated single-agent ablation
matches the routed specialists on $F_2$ with tighter spans. Multi-agent
configurations carry a $23\%$--$67\%$ cost premium uncompensated by $F_2$ gains;
their surviving advantage is schema-enforced verbatim grounding ($100\%$ citation
on predicted clauses, against $54\%$ for zero-shot).

\medskip
\noindent
For legal teams and the software companies that build their AI tools, one
well-prompted model usually reviews contracts as accurately as elaborate
multi-agent systems; the extra agents are only worth their cost when a
regulator or client asks how each clause was found.
