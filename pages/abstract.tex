\chapter{\abstractname}

Contract Lifecycle Management platforms increasingly rely on large language models to
automate clause extraction from commercial contracts.
Both research and vendor communities have advocated multi-agent architectures as a
superior alternative to single-prompt deployments. The empirical case is thin: every existing legal multi-agent system evaluates a single
architecture on a single benchmark, and no published work ablates multi-agent patterns
against single-agent baselines across frontier-model families.
Recent cross-domain evidence (Gao et al.\ 2025; Bogavelli et al.\ 2025)
reports diminishing multi-agent gains as base-model capability rises.

This thesis presents a controlled cross-architecture cross-model comparison of
multi-agent designs for clause extraction on the CUAD benchmark. Five configurations
spanning zero-shot, chain-of-thought, pipeline decomposition, LLM-routed specialists,
and a consolidated single-agent ablation are evaluated across eleven frontier models from
three providers, yielding 163{,}644 paired predictions on the 102-contract CUAD test
split, analysed with McNemar exact tests, paired bootstrap confidence intervals, and
Benjamini--Hochberg correction.

No multi-agent configuration consistently outperforms the strongest single-agent
baseline. Pooled accuracy falls within a $0.011$-point band. Each provider
family peaks under a different configuration --- Anthropic under
chain-of-thought, Google under zero-shot, OpenAI under classify-then-extract
--- and lighter models gain from decomposition where heavier ones do not.
Two mechanisms explain the null result. The pipeline architecture
exhibits a \emph{classification-gate rejection cascade} --- its upstream classifier
silently overrejects on some models --- and the LLM router in the specialist
architecture is empirically redundant: it converges to a deterministic category
lookup and produces lower-quality span outputs than the consolidated ablation.
Multi-agent configurations carry a $23\%$--$67\%$ cost premium without aggregate
accuracy benefit.

The results identify which model--architecture combinations gain, lose, or are
unchanged on $F_2$, and quantify the auditability surplus
(M1: $1.000$ verbatim grounding) that survives the null result.
