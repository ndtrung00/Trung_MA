\chapter{\abstractname}

Contract Lifecycle Management platforms increasingly rely on large language models to
automate clause extraction from commercial contracts.
Both research and vendor communities have advocated multi-agent architectures as a
superior alternative to single-prompt deployments. The empirical case is thin: every existing legal multi-agent system evaluates a single
architecture on a single benchmark, and no published work ablates multi-agent patterns
against single-agent baselines across frontier-model families.
Recent cross-domain evidence further suggests that gains diminish as base-model
capability rises.

This thesis presents a controlled cross-architecture cross-model comparison of
multi-agent designs for clause extraction on the CUAD benchmark. Five configurations
spanning zero-shot, chain-of-thought, pipeline decomposition, LLM-routed specialists,
and a consolidated single-agent ablation are evaluated across eleven frontier models from
three providers, yielding 163{,}644 paired predictions analysed with paired statistical
tests at the natural class distribution.

No multi-agent configuration consistently outperforms the strongest single-agent
baseline. Pooled accuracy across the eleven models falls within a one-point band, and
architectural ranking varies with model scale rather than model family: larger models
perform best under simpler baselines, while lighter models gain from structured
decomposition. Two mechanisms explain the null result. The pipeline architecture exhibits
a \emph{classification-gate rejection cascade} --- its upstream classifier silently
overrejects on some models --- and the LLM router in the specialist architecture is
empirically redundant: it converges to a deterministic category lookup and produces
lower-quality span outputs than the consolidated ablation. Multi-agent configurations
carry a $24\%$--$70\%$ cost premium without aggregate accuracy benefit.

These results delineate when multi-agent designs help, are inert, or actively hurt in
enterprise CLM, and motivate a practitioner-oriented analysis of the gap between
benchmark performance and production deployment.
