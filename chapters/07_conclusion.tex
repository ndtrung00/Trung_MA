\chapter{Conclusion and Outlook}\label{chapter:conclusion}

\section{Summary of Contributions}\label{sec:concl:contributions}

This thesis evaluated five architectural configurations against eleven
frontier proprietary LLMs on the long-contract subset of CUAD, producing
163{,}644 paired sample-level predictions analysed under a controlled
statistical pipeline. The four contributions stated in
Chapter~\ref{chapter:introduction} are restated here with pointers to the
evidence that supports them.

\paragraph{C1 --- Cross-architecture benchmark.}
The first cross-family cross-architecture comparison on CUAD at this scale:
five architectural configurations, eleven frontier models, 55 official runs,
analysed with paired McNemar exact tests, paired bootstrap confidence
intervals, and Benjamini--Hochberg correction. The benchmark, prompt
registry, run artefacts, and statistical pipeline are released alongside the
thesis (Section~\ref{sec:sys:infra}).

\paragraph{C2 --- Capability-tier gradient.}
Architectural ranking is not constant across the model panel. Pooled $F_2$
across the five configurations spans a $0.011$-point band and per-model
winners are split four ways (Table~\ref{tab:h1-f2}). The capability-tier
breakdown (Table~\ref{tab:tier-gradient}) reveals the gradient: light- and
mid-tier models gain from M0's calibration; heavy-tier models are dominated
by B1. No architecture is universally optimal; the choice is conditional on
model family, capability tier, and the chosen $\beta$ of the $F_\beta$
metric.

\paragraph{C3 --- Classification-gate cascade.}
The pipeline-decomposition pattern (M0) suffers a recall--precision exchange
mediated by the classification gate. On Gemini~2.5~Flash and Pro,
$97.8$--$97.9\%$ of M0 false negatives originate at the gate rather than at
the extractor (Table~\ref{tab:cascade}). The exchange's direction is
predictable from a single-agent calibration pass: models with high B1
over-extraction lose recall to the gate; models with low B1 over-extraction
gain $F_1$ from it. Section~\ref{sec:disc:cascade} formalises the heuristic
and connects it to the inter-agent verification gap taxonomy of
\textcite{cemri2025why}.

\paragraph{C4 --- Routing redundancy with span-quality degradation.}
The LLM router agreed with a deterministic category-to-specialist dictionary
on $99.91\%$ of requests across the panel and on $100\%$ for ten of eleven
models. The consolidated single-agent ablation (M6) matches the routed
multi-agent system (M1) on $F_2$ pooled to three decimals, while producing
measurably cleaner spans (Jaccard $+0.071$ on ten of eleven models;
Table~\ref{tab:h3}). In closed-vocabulary specialist-routing designs, the
LLM-mediated routing call adds orchestrator cost without architectural
benefit.

\section{Answers to the Research Questions}\label{sec:concl:rqs}

\paragraph{Main RQ.}
\emph{Can a multi-agent framework improve contract clause extraction accuracy
beyond single-agent baselines while providing superior explainability?}
On accuracy ($F_2$), multi-agent decomposition is dominated at the pooled
level by a zero-shot single agent, and conditionally improves only on the
OpenAI family and on lighter capability tiers. On efficiency
(cost-adjusted $F_2$), multi-agent configurations are Pareto-dominated by B1
deployments on cheaper models (Figure~\ref{fig:pareto}). On explainability,
the pipeline (M0) and routed-specialist (M1) topologies produce the trace
primitives that production review needs
(Section~\ref{sec:auditability}), which is the axis on which the practitioner
argument for multi-agent architectures rests
(Section~\ref{sec:disc:practitioner}).

\paragraph{RQ1 --- Architectural pattern by condition.}
No pattern wins universally. Pooled $F_2$ ranks B1 $>$ B4 $>$ M0 $>$ M1
$\approx$ M6 within a $0.011$-point band. The model-family interaction is
the dominant signal: the Anthropic family peaks under B4, the Google family
under B1, and the OpenAI family under M0. The prevalence-tier interaction
is also large: M1 specialists lead on common-tier categories, M0 on
moderate, and B1 on rare (Table~\ref{tab:tier-winners}). The capability-tier
interaction completes the picture: M0 helps light and mid models, B1 helps
heavy models. The answer to RQ1's ``under what conditions'' clause is
therefore: M0 is preferable when the model is OpenAI-family or light-tier,
$F_1$ is the primary metric, and the practitioner accepts the $1.67\times$
cost premium.

\paragraph{RQ2 --- Multi-agent vs.\ single-agent on accuracy and cost.}
Multi-agent configurations carry mean cost premiums of $1.23\times$ (M1) to
$1.67\times$ (M0) over B1 without compensating $F_2$ gains pooled across the
panel. B1 occupies the Pareto frontier at every cost level
(Figure~\ref{fig:pareto}). The only multi-agent cost premium that pays back
in $F_2$ terms is M0 on the OpenAI family, and even that is dominated in
$F_2$/\$ by a B1 deployment on Gemini~2.5~Flash. On $F_1$, M0 leads pooled
and the premium is partially justified for precision-critical deployments.

\paragraph{RQ3 --- Auditable reasoning traces.}
The parent--child trace structure of M0 and M1 produces decision attribution
--- routing decision, specialist extraction, validation outcome --- that B1
cannot (Section~\ref{sec:auditability}). The practitioner-relevant tradeoffs
are storage cost (approximately $4\times$ the token volume of a B1 call per
M1 run), attribution faithfulness (the trace is faithful to the call graph
but does not certify the model's internal computation), and routing
redundancy (the $99.91\%$ deterministic routing result means the orchestrator
layer adds latency without contributing decision diversity).

\section{Outlook}\label{sec:concl:outlook}

The outlook is organised by three axes on which this work most clearly
under-determines the conclusion: untested architectural patterns, evaluation
beyond CUAD, and cost mechanisms not exercised in the experiments.

\paragraph{Untested architectural patterns.}
The five-configuration grid covers single-agent, pipeline, and
routed-specialist patterns under static and LLM-mediated routing. Three
patterns we expect to perform measurably differently remain unevaluated.
\emph{Reflective verifier agents} \cite{shinn2023reflexion} could absorb
the cascade failure mode of Section~\ref{sec:disc:cascade} by allowing the
extractor to override a silent classifier rejection on confidence-flagged
borderline cases. \emph{Tool-calling specialists} that retrieve clause
exemplars or jurisdictional indicators before extracting are the natural
extension to open-taxonomy production deployments
(Section~\ref{sec:disc:practitioner}, Gap~3). \emph{Hierarchical
orchestration} --- a portfolio-level orchestrator over per-contract
extraction agents --- targets cross-contract reasoning (Gap~5) and is the
architecture we expect to dominate on multi-contract benchmarks that do not
yet exist.

\paragraph{Beyond CUAD.}
Three extensions of the evaluation envelope are immediate.
\emph{Open-weights models} are excluded from the present panel;
the proprietary--open-source gap reported by \textcite{liu2025contracteval}
is the most consequential variable left unexamined, and the
capability-equalisation effect of M0 may be larger on weaker base models,
in which case the architectural ranking observed here inverts.
\emph{Retrieve-then-extract} on a multi-contract corpus is the
production-relevant generalisation of M0 and is currently unbenchmarked
at the scale required (Section~\ref{sec:disc:practitioner}, Gap~2).
\emph{Document-quality stress tests} --- OCR noise, mixed-format documents,
redactions --- would establish whether the architectural ranking is stable
under the preprocessing-degraded inputs that dominate enterprise deployments
(Gap~1).
\emph{Formal user studies} with practising contract reviewers would close
the loop on RQ3 by measuring whether the trace primitives produced by M0
and M1 actually shorten review time or improve exception-triage accuracy
in production, a question this thesis treats as a system property but does
not measure on human reviewers.

\paragraph{Cost mechanisms.}
Two cost mechanisms not exercised here would change the cost picture
(Section~\ref{sec:res:cost}) materially. \emph{Prompt caching}, supported
in production by the major providers in the panel, would substantially
reduce the marginal cost of per-category re-prompting that dominates the
B1 cost structure on long contracts; the $23$--$67\%$ multi-agent cost
premium would shrink correspondingly and the Pareto frontier would
re-form. \emph{Structured-output constraints} would reduce M0
classifier-call latency to the point where the cascade exchange is
acceptable on more models. The Pareto analysis as given is therefore a
snapshot under uniform per-call billing; the deployment-relevant cost
ranking under caching and structured-output decoding is open.

\medskip
\noindent
The thesis's closing position is that the multi-agent design space for CLM
clause extraction is large, that the slice tested here is the slice
production teams are most likely to build first, and that this slice does
not on its own justify multi-agent deployment over a strong single-agent
baseline on accuracy grounds. The case for multi-agent architectures in
production rests on axes the benchmark does not measure: auditability,
modular update boundaries, integration seams, open-taxonomy extensibility,
and cross-contract reasoning. Future work should evaluate those axes
directly, rather than continue to test multi-agent decomposition against
benchmarks where single-agent long-context already absorbs the workload.
