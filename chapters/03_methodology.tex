\chapter{Methodology}\label{chapter:methodology}

This chapter specifies the experimental design used to evaluate multi-agent
architectures for clause extraction in Contract Lifecycle Management (CLM). The chapter
is organised around the research questions introduced in Chapter~1 and the conceptual
framing developed in Chapter~2: we contrast a small set of architectural configurations
on a fixed legal-NLP benchmark, holding model identity and prompt content constant
within each comparison, and use paired statistical procedures to decide between
competing hypotheses about architectural value. Where Chapter~2 surveyed the design
space of agentic systems and the empirical record of LLMs on contract review, this
chapter operationalises that space into testable configurations and a falsification
protocol.

\section{Research Design}\label{sec:meth:design}

The study follows a controlled comparative design in the spirit of
\cite{dror2018hitchhikers} and \cite{card2020little}: a fixed evaluation set, a fixed
metric family, and a small set of clearly-distinguished system configurations whose
differences are intentionally minimised to a single architectural axis at a time. Each
configuration is run, end to end, against the same sample of contracts using the same
eleven LLMs, so that any observed performance difference can be attributed either to
architecture, to the model, or to their interaction, but not to dataset drift or prompt
contamination.

We evaluate five configurations. Two are single-agent baselines: \textbf{B1}, a
zero-shot replication of the ContractEval prompt of \cite{liu2025contracteval} delivered
as a single user message; and \textbf{B4}, a chain-of-thought (CoT) variant that elicits
step-by-step reasoning and a structured final answer. Three are multi-agent
configurations: \textbf{M0}, a classify-then-extract pipeline in which a binary
classifier first decides whether the target category is present and an extractor is
invoked only conditionally; \textbf{M1}, an orchestrator with three domain specialists
(Risk/Liability, Temporal/Renewal, IP/Commercial) and an LLM router; and \textbf{M6},
a single-agent ablation that consolidates the union of all specialist instructions into
one prompt without routing or pipelining. M6 is the architectural control that isolates
the contribution of \emph{routing and decomposition} from the contribution of
\emph{specialist domain knowledge expressed in prompts}.

Three retrieval and verification configurations originally proposed in our
pre-registration (BM25 baseline B5, BM25+reranker B6, extract-then-verify M7, and an
ensemble M8) were cut from the main study to keep the comparison space tractable; we
discuss them as future work in Chapter~7. Holding the design to the five configurations
above preserves the single-variable ablation principle: M0 vs.\ B1 isolates the value
of a classification gate; M1 vs.\ M6 isolates the value of routing and decomposition
given equivalent prompt content; B4 vs.\ B1 isolates the value of explicit reasoning
under a single agent.

Each (configuration, model) cell yields one official run, for a total of
$5 \times 11 = 55$ official runs. Within a run the model evaluates one sample at a time
across all 41 CUAD categories. We log structured traces (Section~\ref{sec:sys:trace})
sufficient to reconstruct every per-call decision \emph{post hoc}.

\section{Dataset Selection and Justification}\label{sec:meth:data}

We use the Contract Understanding Atticus Dataset~\cite{hendrycks2021cuad} as the
evaluation corpus. CUAD is the largest publicly available expert-annotated benchmark
for commercial-contract clause extraction; it contains 510 contracts with 41 categories
of legally salient provisions and is the benchmark used by
ContractEval~\cite{liu2025contracteval} and the majority of recent LLM-based contract
review work surveyed in Chapter~2. Its annotation is performed by attorneys, its license
permits research evaluation, and its category taxonomy aligns with the categories that
legal-tech CLM platforms surface in production review workflows.

For this thesis we restrict evaluation to the 81 CUAD contracts longer than
100{,}000 characters. Two reasons motivate this filter. First, long-context performance
is the regime in which agentic decomposition is hypothesised to add value (Chapter~2):
short contracts can be ingested in a single prompt by every model in our study, so any
architectural differentiation should be most visible on long ones. Second, long contracts
approximate the operational distribution of CLM platforms more closely than short addenda
or one-page NDAs that dominate the lower tail of CUAD. The 81-contract subset yields a
raw enumeration of $81 \times 41 = 3{,}321$ (contract, category) pairs per evaluation
pass. After per-run downsampling (Section~\ref{sec:meth:sampling}) each run produces
approximately 2{,}999 predictions; aggregated across the 55 official runs we analyse
163{,}644 sample-level predictions after dropping 1{,}297 samples
($\approx 0.79\%$) for which the model returned a malformed classification token
(Section~\ref{sec:meth:stats}).

Following CUAD's evaluation convention, ground truth is the union of
attorney-annotated answer spans for each (contract, category) pair. A category for which
the annotators marked no span is a true negative (the clause is genuinely absent). This
convention preserves the natural imbalance of the corpus, which we discuss next.

\section{Sampling Strategy: Natural Class Distribution}\label{sec:meth:sampling}

CUAD is naturally imbalanced toward absence: most categories are absent from most
contracts. Pilot work for this thesis initially used an \texttt{include\_negative=True}
configuration that produced an approximately $67\%$ positive~/ $33\%$ negative split
per run. We replaced this with a \texttt{neg\_ratio=0.7} sampler that produces a $30\%$
positive~/ $70\%$ negative split, mirroring the natural class prior in the long-contract
subset. Switching to the natural prior makes one failure mode visible that the
inflated-positive sampler had masked: under a positive-heavy sample, over-extraction
(a model emitting text for a category that is in fact absent) is rarely penalised because
there are few absent samples; under the natural prior, over-extraction directly depresses
precision and is reflected in $F$-measures. We retain the natural-prior sampler for all
reported results.

Each run draws samples with a fixed pseudo-random seed (\texttt{seed=42}) so that the
same (contract, category) pairs are evaluated across configurations within a model. This
is the basis for paired comparison: every statistical test is computed on the
intersection of sample IDs across the two configurations being compared, never on
independent draws. Where partial runs differ in coverage (rare), the intersection is
taken explicitly and reported.

To assess whether architectures behave differently on rare versus common provisions,
we partition the 41 CUAD categories into three prevalence \emph{tiers} computed from
the natural positive rate of each category in the long-contract subset: \emph{common},
\emph{moderate}, and \emph{rare}. The tier cutpoints are fixed before any model is run
and are the same across all configurations and models. Per-tier metrics
(Section~\ref{sec:meth:metrics}) are then reported alongside the pooled metric.

\section{Metric Selection}\label{sec:meth:metrics}

Clause extraction has two natural granularities. At sample level, it is a binary
classification problem (\emph{is the clause present in this contract?}). At span level,
it is an extraction problem (\emph{which substring expresses the clause?}). We report
metrics at both granularities.

The primary metric is the $F_\beta$ score with $\beta = 2$, which weights recall four
times as heavily as precision. The choice reflects the asymmetry of legal review: a
missed risky clause is a substantive harm to the downstream user (an attorney or
contracting party), whereas a falsely flagged clause is corrected cheaply by the
reviewer in the next stage of the CLM workflow. This recall-favouring asymmetry is
consistent with how earlier CUAD work has framed
evaluation~\cite{hendrycks2021cuad,liu2025contracteval}, and we treat $F_2$ as the
default for hypothesis testing.

We also report standard $F_1$ as a comparison-friendly secondary metric, precision and
recall separately to expose the failure mode behind any $F_\beta$ change, and span-level
Jaccard overlap to capture the quality of extracted spans conditional on extraction. Two
complementary diagnostic rates are tracked: the \emph{laziness rate}, defined as the
fraction of clause-present samples for which the model output is ``no related clause''
(i.e.\ silent omission), and its dual, the \emph{over-extraction rate}, defined as the
fraction of clause-absent samples for which the model returned a non-empty span
(i.e.\ hallucinated or over-eager flagging). All metrics are reported pooled across the
41 CUAD categories and broken down per tier (common, moderate, rare).

\section{Hypotheses and Success Criteria}\label{sec:meth:hypotheses}

We pre-register three hypotheses corresponding to the three sub-research-questions of
Chapter~1. Each is stated neutrally as a falsifiable proposition; the chapter does not
pre-commit to a result.

\paragraph{H1 (Architectural value).}
\emph{There exists at least one multi-agent configuration
$C \in \{\text{M0}, \text{M1}, \text{M6}\}$ that outperforms the strongest single-agent
baseline $B^\star \in \{\text{B1}, \text{B4}\}$ on $F_2$.} The null is that no such $C$
exists. The test statistic is McNemar's discordant-pair count between $B^\star$ and $C$,
computed on per-sample classification correctness ($\mathrm{TP} \cup \mathrm{TN}$
treated as ``correct''); we use the exact binomial form via
\texttt{scipy.stats.binomtest}~\cite{mcnemar1947note,virtanen2020scipy}. The decision
rule is rejection of the null if the BH-corrected two-sided $p$-value falls below
$\alpha = 0.05$ on at least one such pair, with the $F_2$ direction favouring $C$. The
effect size reported alongside is the per-model paired bootstrap mean $\Delta F_2$ with
300 resamples and a $95\%$ percentile confidence
interval~\cite{efron1979bootstrap}.

\paragraph{H2 (Tier-equity).}
\emph{Multi-agent architectures reduce the rare--common $F_2$ gap relative to
single-agent baselines.} Define
$\mathrm{Gap}(C) = F_2^{\text{common}}(C) - F_2^{\text{rare}}(C)$
for configuration $C$. The null is
$\mathrm{Gap}(C_{\text{multi}}) \geq \mathrm{Gap}(B^\star)$.
Inference uses a per-tier paired bootstrap on the gap difference
$\Delta\mathrm{Gap} = \mathrm{Gap}(C_{\text{multi}}) - \mathrm{Gap}(B^\star)$ with 300
resamples and $95\%$ percentile CI; the null is rejected if the upper end of the CI
lies below zero.

\paragraph{H3 (Architecture vs.\ prompts).}
\emph{The benefit (if any) of M1 over a single-agent baseline is attributable to the
architectural decomposition itself, beyond what is achievable by consolidating specialist
instructions into a single prompt (M6).} The null is that M1 and M6 are statistically
indistinguishable. The primary test is two-sided McNemar's exact on M1 vs.\ M6
per-sample correctness; a paired-bootstrap $\Delta F_2$ confidence interval is reported
as effect size. As a secondary test we compare M0 vs.\ M6 to separate the contribution
of the classification gate from that of decomposition. Span-level Jaccard is reported
alongside as a quality-of-output check.

For all three hypotheses we report pooled and per-tier statistics; H1 and H3 use
BH-corrected $p$-values within their family of pairwise comparisons
(Section~\ref{sec:meth:stats}). The pre-registered family for H1 is the ten pooled
pairwise comparisons induced by the five configurations (i.e.\ $\binom{5}{2} = 10$). H2
is reported as effect-size CIs without an additional FDR correction because it is a
single directional contrast per (configuration, baseline) pair on a different statistic
than H1.

\section{Statistical Methodology}\label{sec:meth:stats}

\paragraph{Per-sample paired tests.}
For each pair of configurations being compared, we compute per-sample correctness as
the indicator that the model's binary classification matches the ground truth (TP or
TN). We then form the McNemar discordant pair counts $b$ (correct under $A$, incorrect
under $B$) and $c$ (incorrect under $A$, correct under $B$) and obtain the two-sided
exact $p$-value as the binomial tail probability under $\mathrm{Binomial}(b+c, 0.5)$
\cite{mcnemar1947note}, implemented via
\texttt{scipy.stats.binomtest}~\cite{virtanen2020scipy}. The exact binomial form is
preferred over the chi-squared approximation because some discordant counts on
rare-category subsets are small.

\paragraph{Paired bootstrap.}
For continuous metrics ($F_2$, $F_1$, Jaccard, gap differences) we use the paired
non-parametric bootstrap~\cite{efron1979bootstrap,riezler2005pitfalls}. Sample IDs
(not categories) are resampled with replacement; both configurations are evaluated on
the resampled IDs to preserve pairing. We use $B = 300$ resamples and report the
$95\%$ percentile confidence interval. Per-tier bootstraps for H2 resample within tier
strata. The number of resamples (300) was chosen to balance Monte Carlo error against
compute cost across 55 runs; we report bootstrap stability as a robustness check in
Chapter~5.

\paragraph{Multiple-comparison correction.}
The H1 family is the ten pooled pairwise configuration comparisons, evaluated within
each model. We apply the Benjamini--Hochberg
procedure~\cite{benjamini1995controlling} at $\alpha = 0.05$ to control the false
discovery rate within this family. We report both raw and BH-corrected $p$-values to
allow the reader to apply alternative thresholds.

\paragraph{Paired comparison protocol.}
Every test is computed on the intersection of sample IDs between the two configurations,
never on independent samples. When a model's run is partially missing for some sample
IDs (e.g.\ because of provider rate limits), the missing samples are dropped
\emph{symmetrically} from both configurations being compared. The intersection size is
reported with each test.

\paragraph{Missing-data handling.}
Some model outputs return a malformed classification token (neither a clean ``yes/no''
nor a parseable span). Such samples cannot be assigned a TP/FP/TN/FN label and are
excluded from per-sample tests. Across the entire study this affects 1{,}297 of
164{,}941 predictions, or $0.79\%$. The exclusion is applied symmetrically per pairwise
comparison: if a sample is null in either of the two configurations being compared, it
is dropped from that comparison only. This avoids penalising the configuration that
happened to produce a parseable answer.

\paragraph{Statistical power.}
\cite{card2020little} caution that NLP comparisons are frequently under-powered. The
pairwise sample size after intersection is on the order of $2{,}900$--$3{,}000$ per
(configuration, model) pair, which is comparable to or larger than the GLUE test sets
analysed in their meta-study and is sufficient to detect small-to-moderate effects in
$F_2$ with the bootstrap procedure used here. We do not, however, claim power against
arbitrarily small effects; the smallest effect size detectable in our design is reported
alongside each comparison.

\section{Model Selection}\label{sec:meth:models}

We evaluate eleven frontier proprietary models drawn from three families: Anthropic
(Claude Haiku~4.5, Claude Sonnet~4, Claude Sonnet~4.6, Claude Opus~4.6); Google
(Gemini~2.5 Flash, Gemini~2.5 Pro, Gemini~3 Flash, Gemini~3.1 Pro); and OpenAI
(GPT-4.1, GPT-4.1 Mini, GPT-4.1 Nano). The selection covers three deployed model
families and, within each family, spans a deliberate compute-tier range from light
(Haiku, Flash, Nano) to heavy (Opus, Pro). The breadth lets us separate effects that
are model-family-specific (e.g.\ a particular family responding well to
chain-of-thought) from effects that are architectural in origin. We restrict the main
analysis to proprietary models because they represent the deployment substrate of
current commercial CLM platforms, which is the population to which the thesis intends
to generalise; an open-weights extension is discussed in Chapter~7.

\section{Threats to Validity}\label{sec:meth:threats}

\paragraph{Internal validity.}
Configuration parity is the central internal-validity concern. We mitigate it by holding
the underlying CUAD loader, sampling seed, prompt registry, and trace schema constant
across configurations, and by isolating prompts: the same specialist instructions appear
in M1 (split across three agents) and M6 (concatenated in one agent), so the textual
prompt content is the same union in both cases. Temperature and decoding parameters are
fixed per provider. The chain-of-thought parsing logic for B4 went through one revision
(an early delimiter-based parser was replaced by structured-output enforcement; see
Section~\ref{sec:sys:baselines}), and only the post-fix version is included in the
official runs.

\paragraph{External validity.}
The evaluation is single-language (English), single-jurisdiction (predominantly
U.S.\ commercial contracts), and single-corpus (CUAD). Generalisation to other legal
traditions, languages, or contract types is not guaranteed by these results and is
discussed explicitly in Chapter~7. We also use a fixed snapshot of each model; provider
model updates can shift behaviour, and we record exact model identifiers in the run
metadata.

\paragraph{Construct validity.}
$F_2$ is a proxy for legal review utility, not a measurement of it. The legal-risk
asymmetry that motivates $\beta = 2$ is plausible but not measured against actual
reviewer behaviour, and reviewer cost of inspecting an over-extracted span is
workflow-dependent. We mitigate this by reporting precision, recall, Jaccard, laziness,
and over-extraction independently so a reader with a different utility model can
re-weight.

\paragraph{Statistical validity.}
Three concerns deserve flagging. First, multiple comparisons inflate false-positive risk;
we control the family-wise FDR via BH~\cite{benjamini1995controlling} but not all
secondary contrasts are corrected, and we report this transparently. Second, model
identity is treated as a fixed factor rather than a random effect, which limits inference
to the eleven models studied. Third, bootstrap percentile CIs are known to under-cover
for small samples and skewed statistics~\cite{efron1979bootstrap}; we report bootstrap
diagnostics in Chapter~5 and treat tail-area $p$-values as decisive only when
corroborated by the McNemar exact test.
