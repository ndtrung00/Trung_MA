\chapter{Background and Related Work}\label{chapter:literature-review}

\section{Background}\label{sec:lit:background}

\subsection{Contract Lifecycle Management}\label{sec:lit:clm}

CLM is the end-to-end workflow through which an organisation authors, negotiates,
executes, monitors, and retires its contractual instruments.
Figure~\ref{fig:clm-lifecycle} shows the canonical six-stage model.
The economic surface is large: $\approx\!9\%$ annual revenue loss from poor
contracting~\cite{worldcc2025benchmark}, with 44\% of U.S.\ legal-task time exposed
to automation~\cite{goldmansachs2023ai}. Three NLP sub-tasks dominate the
Execute and Monitor stages: \emph{clause extraction} (locate and bound the span
instantiating a clause category), \emph{risk identification} (classify extracted
clauses against playbook standards), and \emph{obligation tracking} (convert executed
clauses into a structured event calendar).

\subsection{Large Language Models}\label{sec:lit:llm-background}

An LLM is a neural language model parameterised by $\theta$ that factorises a token
sequence $\mathbf{x} = (x_1,\dots,x_n)$ autoregressively:
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:llm}
  P(\mathbf{x};\theta) = \prod_{t=1}^{n} P(x_t \mid x_1,\dots,x_{t-1};\theta).
\end{equation}
%
Training minimises the negative log-likelihood
$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = -\sum_{t=1}^{n} \log P(x_t \mid x_{<t};\theta)$
over large text corpora. At inference the model generates $x_t$ by sampling from the
distribution over the vocabulary conditioned on the preceding context. Instruction
tuning and reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) align the base model
toward task-following behaviour without changing the fundamental
factorisation~\cite{devlin2019bert}.

\subsection{Agentic AI and Multi-Agent Systems}\label{sec:lit:mas-background}

An \emph{agent} is an LLM invocation that (i) receives a structured role specification
and context, (ii) may call tools or sub-agents, and (iii) emits a typed output.
A \emph{multi-agent system} (MAS) is a directed graph of agents with typed
message-passing edges; see Figure~\ref{fig:mas-architecture}.
Key structural dimensions are topology (hierarchical vs.\ peer), routing (static
pipeline vs.\ LLM-mediated dispatch), specialisation granularity (per-task vs.\
per-domain vs.\ per-step), and verification structure (none, reflective, debate, or
judge-agent)~\cite{wu2023autogen,hong2024metagpt,li2023camel}.

\subsection{The CUAD Benchmark}\label{sec:lit:cuad}

CUAD~\cite{hendrycks2021cuad} is the standard public benchmark for commercial-contract
clause extraction (Table~\ref{tab:cuad-stats}). Each (contract, category) pair is
labelled with zero or more verbatim attorney-annotated spans; absence of a span is a
true negative. Category prevalence is heavily skewed toward absence ($\approx\!70\%$
negative at the instance level), which is why sampling design is a methodological
lever (Section~\ref{sec:meth:sampling}).

\section{Contract Analysis Methods}\label{sec:lit:methods}

\paragraph{Rule-based and statistical methods.}
Pre-LLM extraction used regular expressions, gazetteers, and
CRFs~\cite{lafferty2001crf}. High precision on narrow clause types degraded on
out-of-template contracts. BiLSTM-CRF~\cite{lample2016neural} improved
cross-document robustness; BERT-class encoders~\cite{devlin2019bert} then replaced
recurrent encoders with self-attention, enabling fine-tuning on small legal corpora.
LEGAL-BERT~\cite{chalkidis2020legalbert} showed domain pre-training improves
downstream legal NLP performance.

\paragraph{LLM-based methods.}
BERT-class models are limited to 512 tokens, require labelled data per clause
category, and do not generalise zero-shot. LLMs address all three limitations.
Martin et al.~\cite{martin2024bettercallgpt} report GPT-4-1106 at $F\!=\!0.87$ on
issue determination, matching legal-process outsourcers at $\approx\!1/100$ the cost
(vendor-funded; directional finding replicated independently~\cite{liu2025contracteval}).
ContractEval~\cite{liu2025contracteval} benchmarks 19 LLMs on CUAD; proprietary
models outperform open-source, scaling yields diminishing returns for open-source
models, and reasoning-mode prompting improves span quality but degrades binary
correctness. Open-source models exhibit a \emph{laziness} failure: defaulting to
``no related clause'' even when the clause is present.

\section{Conditions for Multi-Agent Effectiveness}\label{sec:lit:when}

Three recent studies directly bound the scope of multi-agent benefits.
Gao et al.~\cite{gao2025single} show the advantage diminishes for frontier models
(o3, Gemini~2.5~Pro) whose long-context reasoning absorbs workloads that previously
required decomposition. Cemri et al.~\cite{cemri2025why} identify 14 MAST failure
modes in $>$1{,}600 traces across 7 frameworks; simple interventions yield only
14--15\% recovery, suggesting structural rather than prompt-level causes.
Bogavelli et al.~\cite{bogavelli2025agentarch} (AgentArch: 18 configurations,
6 frontier models) find no universally optimal architecture --- best-of-class reaches
only 35.3\% on harder enterprise tasks --- and ReAct-style prompting produces
hallucinated tool calls in multi-agent settings.
Sanwal~\cite{sanwal2025layered} proposes external verification at each reasoning
layer to address unverified intermediate steps in chain-of-thought pipelines.
Taken together: multi-agent designs help when tasks are decomposable, specialists
meaningfully outperform a generalist, and the underlying model is weak enough that
decomposition provides a lever. This thesis empirically tests whether these conditions
hold for clause-level CUAD extraction.

\section{Evaluation Methodology}\label{sec:lit:eval}

\paragraph{Metrics.}
Let TP, FP, FN, TN be per-sample counts, $S$ the predicted span, $T$ the reference
span. Formal definitions:
%
\begin{align}
  F_\beta &= \frac{(1+\beta^2)\,\text{TP}}{(1+\beta^2)\,\text{TP}
            + \beta^2\,\text{FN} + \text{FP}}, \quad \beta \in \{1,2\}
            \label{eq:fbeta} \\[4pt]
  J(S,T) &= \frac{|S \cap T|}{|S \cup T|}
            \label{eq:jaccard} \\[4pt]
  \lambda &= \frac{|\{i : y_i{=}1,\; \hat{y}_i{=}0\}|}{|\{i : y_i{=}1\}|}
            \qquad \text{(laziness rate)}
            \label{eq:laziness} \\[4pt]
  \omega  &= \frac{|\{i : y_i{=}0,\; \hat{y}_i{=}1\}|}{|\{i : y_i{=}0\}|}
            \qquad \text{(over-extraction rate)}
            \label{eq:overextract}
\end{align}
%
$\beta{=}2$ weights recall four times as heavily as precision, reflecting the
legal-risk asymmetry where missed clauses incur downstream financial
exposure~\cite{hendrycks2021cuad,liu2025contracteval}. Jaccard is reported at span
level conditional on extraction.

\paragraph{Statistical testing.}
Dror et al.~\cite{dror2018hitchhikers} is the canonical reference. For paired binary
outcomes McNemar's exact test~\cite{mcnemar1947note} is used; for continuous metrics
the paired non-parametric bootstrap~\cite{efron1979bootstrap,riezler2005pitfalls}
with 95\% percentile CI. False discovery rate is controlled via
Benjamini--Hochberg~\cite{benjamini1995controlling} across the 10 pairwise
configuration comparisons ($\alpha{=}0.05$). A single sampling design and prompt
template is held fixed across all models; prompt sensitivity is treated as a
validity threat (Section~\ref{sec:meth:threats}).

\paragraph{Sampling.}
CUAD's natural clause-presence rate is $\approx\!30\%$ positive at the instance level.
Evaluations that inflate this ratio produce optimistic precision
estimates~\cite{liu2025contracteval}. This thesis samples at the natural ratio,
exposing over-extraction as a primary failure mode.

\section{Research Gap}\label{sec:lit:synthesis}

No published study simultaneously (i) cross-varies multi-agent architecture and
frontier-model family on CUAD, (ii) samples at the natural class distribution, (iii)
applies paired statistical tests with multiple-comparison correction, and (iv) ablates
the multi-agent design against a consolidated single-agent prompt. This thesis
addresses all four requirements.
