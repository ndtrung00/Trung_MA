\chapter{Results}\label{chapter:results}

This chapter reports the empirical results of the 55-run grid specified in
Chapter~\ref{chapter:methodology}. Section~\ref{sec:res:overview} gives the
sample accounting. Sections~\ref{sec:res:h1}--\ref{sec:res:h3} test the three
pre-registered hypotheses. Sections~\ref{sec:res:cascade} and \ref{sec:res:cost}
report two findings that bear directly on the research questions: the
classification-gate cascade (contribution~C3) and the cost-accuracy frontier
underwriting~RQ2. Section~\ref{sec:res:rq-summary} answers RQ1 and RQ2 at the
level of evidence established here; deeper interpretation is reserved for
Chapter~\ref{chapter:discussion}.

\paragraph{H1 verdict.}
H1 is supported in conditional form. The pre-registered decision rule (reject
$H_0$ if any multi-agent vs.\ B1 McNemar contrast is BH-significant with
$\Delta F_2 > 0$) is met: fifteen of the 22 per-model contrasts in
Table~\ref{tab:h1-stats} are positive. However, support is family-specific,
not universal. For the OpenAI family, M0 outperforms B1 by $+0.052$ pooled on
$F_2$, and GPT-4.1\,Nano improves by $+0.171$. For the Anthropic family, M0
and B4 outperform B1 on lighter models (Sonnet\,4: $+0.025$;
Sonnet\,4.6: $+0.047$). For the Google family, H1 is not supported: B1
dominates M0 by $0.078$ points (Gemini\,2.5\,Flash: $-0.210$;
Gemini\,2.5\,Pro: $-0.121$), and M1 fails to improve over B1 on all four
Google models. Pooled $F_2$ is within a $0.011$-point band; the model-family
interaction is the dominant signal. On $F_1$, M0 leads all configurations
pooled ($0.798$ vs.\ $0.737$ for B1), reversing the $F_2$ ranking and
reframing the architectural choice as a metric-dependent decision
(Section~\ref{sec:res:rq-summary}).

\paragraph{H2 verdict.}
H2 is rejected for the multi-agent configurations M0 and M1: both widen the
rare--common gap relative to B1. The widening is driven by two distinct
mechanisms. Under M0, the classification gate (Section~\ref{sec:res:cascade})
collapses rare-tier $F_2$ on the Gemini family (Gemini\,2.5\,Flash: $F_2^r$

specialists fail catastrophically on rare categories (GPT-4.1: $F_2^r$ drops
$0.704 \to 0.430$; GPT-4.1\,Nano: $0.253 \to 0.167$). The B4 chain-of-thought
baseline and M6 consolidated specialist do reduce the gap (means 0.240 and
0.226), but neither is a multi-agent architecture in the sense of
Chapter~\ref{chapter:methodology}.

\section{H3 --- Architecture vs.\ Prompt Content (M1 vs.\ M6)}\label{sec:res:h3}

The M1 router agreed with the deterministic category-to-specialist mapping on
$100.00\%$ of requests for ten of eleven models, and on $99.03\%$ for
GPT-4.1\,Nano (29 mis-routes out of 2{,}999), yielding $99.91\%$ routing
accuracy across the panel. Routing is empirically a deterministic lookup: the
router's decisions can be replicated by a static dictionary mapping category
names to specialist labels at zero LLM cost. We therefore present no figure for
routing accuracy and treat M1 vs.\ M6 as a test of whether dispatch architecture
--- with routing held empirically constant --- adds value over a single-agent
prompt containing the same specialist text.

Despite identical routing, M1 and M6 are statistically distinguishable on $F_2$
for all 11 models after BH correction (McNemar two-sided, $\alpha{=}0.05$,
$m{=}33$). The direction is inconsistent: M1 outperforms M6 on 5 models, M6 on

\paragraph{H3 verdict.}
H3 is supported at the per-model level: M1 and M6 are statistically
distinguishable on $F_2$ for all 11 models after BH correction (McNemar
two-sided, $\alpha{=}0.05$). The direction of the difference, however, is
inconsistent --- M1 outperforms M6 on 5 models, M6 on 6 --- and pooled $F_2$
is identical to three decimal places ($0.790$ vs.\ $0.790$). H3 therefore
confirms that dispatch architecture alters model behaviour, not that it
improves it. The direction inconsistency is itself a finding: routing overhead
hurts precision-oriented models (GPT-family, $\Delta F_2 {=} -0.056$ to
$-0.076$) and helps recall-deficient ones (Gemini\,3\,Flash,
$\Delta F_2 {=} +0.138$). On the secondary axis of span quality, M6 dominates
10 of 11 models with a $+0.071$ Jaccard advantage (contribution~C4): the
single-agent consolidated prompt produces tighter, more precise extractions
than the routed specialist pipeline. A deterministic lookup replaces the LLM
router without measurable $F_2$ loss.

\section{Classification-Gate Cascade}\label{sec:res:cascade}

Table~\ref{tab:cascade} quantifies the M0 cascade. The M0 classifier's

\paragraph{The cascade is a recall--precision exchange, not pure regression.}
The complement of lost recall is gained precision. Table~\ref{tab:omega} reports

most ($\omega$ drops from $0.352$ to $0.099$) but pays the steepest recall
cost. M0 therefore trades recall for precision via the gate. The trade is
favourable on $F_1$ (M0 leads pooled, Table~\ref{tab:h1-headline}) and
unfavourable on $F_2$ (B1 leads pooled). Which side of the trade is preferable
depends on the deployment cost ratio of missed clauses vs.\ false extractions,
a question revisited in Chapter~\ref{chapter:discussion}.

\paragraph{Cascade predictability.}
The cascade severity correlates with the base model's B1 over-extraction rate:
the more liberally a model extracts under B1, the more recall it surrenders at
the gate. Gemini\,2.5\,Pro ($\omega_{\text{B1}} = 0.456$) loses $0.121$ $F_2$

$0.171$ $F_2$ points, because the gate corrects its B1 laziness ($55.5\%$
FN-rate). A practitioner can predict whether M0 will help or hurt a given
model by running a cheap B1 calibration pass and inspecting $\omega_{\text{B1}}$
against the FN-rate profile.

\section{Contract-Length Moderation}\label{sec:res:length}

The cascade analysis (Section~\ref{sec:res:cascade}) and tier analysis
(Table~\ref{tab:tier-winners}) treat all contracts uniformly. Contract length
provides an orthogonal moderating variable: the context-window motivation for
multi-agent decomposition predicts that routing clauses to specialists should
help on \emph{longer} contracts, where a monolithic prompt must attend over
more irrelevant text. We test this prediction by splitting the 102 CUAD
contracts into quartiles by character count and computing per-configuration
$F_2$ within each quartile.

Table~\ref{tab:length-quartile} shows the opposite of the predicted pattern.
On Q1 contracts (under 14{,}384 characters), M1 leads by $+3.7$ percentage
points over B1 and M0 by $+2.0$ points. On Q4 contracts (over 62{,}272
characters), B1 leads by $+3.2$ points over M0 and $+4.1$ over M1. The
crossing point lies between Q2 and Q3 (${\approx}25{,}900$ characters). The
mechanism is plausibly gate-induced: on long contracts the M0 classifier is
exposed to larger token windows, increasing the probability of conservative
\texttt{predicted\_no\_clause} decisions that trigger the cascade documented
in Section~\ref{sec:res:cascade}. For M1, long-contract degradation may
reflect specialist prompt dilution when many clause types from one domain
appear in a single long document. In either case, multi-agent decomposition
as implemented offers no context-window advantage over a zero-shot prompt on
CUAD-class contract lengths.

\section{Answers to Research Questions}\label{sec:res:rq-summary}

\paragraph{RQ1 (which architectural pattern wins, and under what conditions).}
No architectural pattern wins universally on $F_2$. Pooled $F_2$ ranks within a
$0.011$-point band, and per-model winners are split four ways across the panel.
The dominant signal is the model-family interaction: the Anthropic family peaks
at B4 ($\overline{F_2} = 0.851$), the Google family at B1 ($0.826$), and the
OpenAI family at M0 ($0.783$). The choice of evaluation metric also flips the
ranking: M0 dominates the panel on $F_1$ ($0.798$ vs.\ $0.737$ for B1) by
suppressing over-extraction. The condition under which a multi-agent pipeline
(M0) is preferable is therefore: the model is OpenAI-family, the practitioner
accepts the $1.67\times$ cost premium, and precision ($F_1$) is the primary
criterion. All other conditions favour simpler baselines.

\paragraph{RQ2 (cost-effectiveness vs.\ single-agent baselines).}
B1 occupies the Pareto frontier at every cost level. Multi-agent configurations
carry mean cost premiums of $1.23\times$ (M1) to $1.67\times$ (M0) without
compensating $F_2$ gains pooled across the panel. The only cost premium that
partially pays back is M0 on the OpenAI family ($+0.052$ $F_2$ over B1), but
even that is dominated in $F_2$/\$ by cheaper B1 deployments on the Google
family. Cost-aware deployment of clause extraction at CUAD-class scale should
default to single-agent zero-shot on a small, fast model unless the deployment
context provides specific reasons to prefer higher precision over recall.

\paragraph{RQ3 (auditable reasoning traces).}
Addressed by system design in Chapter~\ref{chapter:system-design}. The M1
trace structure produces explicit parent-child decision attribution --- routing
decision, specialist extraction, validation outcome --- that B1 does not.
Three concrete tradeoffs attend this auditability surplus. First, token storage:
each M1 call emits a LangFuse trace of approximately $4\times$ the token volume
of a B1 call (orchestrator hop plus three specialist sub-prompts logged at
inference time), raising persistent storage requirements proportionally.
Second, attribution faithfulness: LangFuse records the \emph{submitted} prompt
and \emph{returned} completion but cannot verify that the model's stated
reasoning reflects its actual inference process; the trace is auditable but not
interpretable in a mechanistic sense. Third, routing redundancy: because the
M1 router is empirically a deterministic lookup
(Section~\ref{sec:res:h3}, $99.91\%$ accuracy), the orchestrator layer adds
latency and cost without contributing decision diversity. Whether the
auditability surplus justifies these costs is a deployment question revisited in
Chapter~\ref{chapter:discussion}.
