\section{H1 --- Multi-Agent vs.\ Single-Agent Baselines}\label{sec:res:h1}

This section presents the central H1 evidence: per-model McNemar exact tests on the
family of pairwise configuration comparisons, paired bootstrap $\Delta F_2$ effect
sizes, and the BH-corrected $p$-value table. The headline finding is that no multi-agent
configuration consistently outperforms the strongest single-agent baseline on $F_2$
across models: pooled mean $F_2$ across the eleven models lies within a 1.1-point band
($\mathrm{B1} = 0.801$, $\mathrm{B4} = 0.796$, $\mathrm{M0} = 0.794$,
$\mathrm{M1} = 0.790$, $\mathrm{M6} = 0.790$). The verdict on H1 is therefore
mixed/conditional rather than supportive in the pooled view, while a model-family
interaction is documented (Claude-family models prefer B4, Gemini-family prefer B1,
OpenAI-family prefer M0). M0 wins on 5 of 11 models against B1 and loses on 4 of 11.

\section{Additional Findings}\label{sec:res:additional}

\subsection{The Near-Perfect Routing Accuracy Critique}\label{sec:res:routing}

This subsection examines the apparently strong but methodologically suspicious finding
that M1's LLM router agrees with the fixed category-to-specialist mapping at $100\%$ on
10 of 11 models. The finding is framed as evidence \emph{against} the diagnostic value
of router accuracy: when the routing task collapses to a category-name classification
with disjoint, well-named clusters, frontier LLMs solve it trivially, and router
accuracy ceases to discriminate among architectures. A deterministic dictionary lookup
would achieve the same routing outcome at zero LLM cost.

\subsection{The Classification-Gate Rejection Cascade}\label{sec:res:cascade}

This subsection documents M0's dominant failure mode: the binary classifier overrejects
on certain models, causing the extractor to be bypassed and the system to silently emit
``no related clause''. On Gemini~2.5 Flash the wrong-rejection rate is $40.9\%$ versus
$2.7\%$ for B1; on Gemini~2.5 Pro it is $31.7\%$ versus $1.5\%$. Across all 11 models,
between $70\%$ and $98\%$ of M0's false negatives are attributable to
\texttt{predicted\_no\_clause} classifier verdicts.

\subsection{Cost-Efficiency Analysis}\label{sec:res:cost}

Multi-agent configurations cost $24\%$--$70\%$ more than B1 per run with no aggregate
$F_2$ benefit, placing B1 on the Pareto frontier in the ($F_2$, cost) plane for the
eleven models studied. This is a deployment-relevant finding rather than a refutation
of agentic architectures in principle: the cost premium may be justified in workflows
where the per-agent traces add operational value (e.g.\ obligation-tracking systems
that need to surface the reasoning path to a reviewer). The conditions under which the
premium is justified are discussed in Chapter~6.
