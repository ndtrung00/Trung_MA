supported for some Anthropic and OpenAI models.

GPT-family specialist failure on rare categories.

Near-perfect routing accuracy is in fact evidence \emph{against} the diagnostic
value of routing: the task reduces to a category-name lookup with three disjoint,
descriptively-named specialist domains. A deterministic dictionary would achieve the
same outcome at zero LLM cost.

Despite identical routing, M1 and M6 are statistically distinguishable on $F_2$ for
all 11 models after BH correction. The direction, however, is inconsistent: M1
outperforms M6 on 5 models (Haiku\,4.5, Opus\,4.6, Gemini\,2.5\,Pro,
Gemini\,3\,Flash, GPT-4.1\,Nano), and M6 outperforms M1 on 6 models (Sonnet\,4,
Sonnet\,4.6, Gemini\,2.5\,Flash, Gemini\,3.1\,Pro, GPT-4.1, GPT-4.1\,Mini). The
largest absolute differences are Gemini\,3\,Flash ($+0.138$ for M1) and GPT-4.1
($-0.076$ for M6), suggesting a model-capacity interaction: lighter/flash models
benefit from the smaller individual specialist prompts, while capable heavy models
handle the consolidated prompt more effectively.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{H3 verdict.}
Rejected. M1 and M6 are statistically distinguishable on $F_2$ for all 11 models
($p < 0.05$ BH-corrected). However, the direction is not consistent across model
families. There is no systematic benefit of the routing architecture over the
consolidated specialist prompt. The pooled means coincide ($\overline{F_2} = 0.790$
for both), and the per-model differences are explained by a model-capacity interaction
rather than by routing contribution.

\section{Additional Findings}\label{sec:res:additional}

\subsection{The Classification-Gate Rejection Cascade}\label{sec:res:cascade}

Table~\ref{tab:cascade} quantifies the M0 cascade failure. The M0 classifier emits
\texttt{predicted\_no\_clause} (a silent empty return, bypassing the extractor) on a
fraction of positive instances. The \emph{gate-caused FN share} measures what
fraction of M0 false negatives originate from this path.

\begin{table}[h]
  \centering
  \small
  \begin{tabular}{lccccc}
    \toprule
    \textbf{Model}
    & \textbf{M0 laz.} & \textbf{B1 laz.} & \textbf{M0 FN}
    & \textbf{Gate-FN} & \textbf{Gate \%} \\
    \midrule
    Claude Haiku 4.5   & 16.9\% &  3.9\% & 255 & 210 & 82.4\% \\
    Claude Opus 4.6    &  7.6\% &  1.9\% & 142 &  95 & 66.9\% \\
    Claude Sonnet 4    &  7.0\% &  2.1\% & 123 &  87 & 70.7\% \\
    Claude Sonnet 4.6  & 10.5\% &  2.0\% & 156 & 131 & 84.0\% \\
    Gemini 2.5 Flash   & 40.9\% &  2.7\% & 520 & 509 & 97.9\% \\
    Gemini 2.5 Pro     & 31.7\% &  1.5\% & 403 & 394 & 97.8\% \\
    Gemini 3 Flash     & 17.9\% &  2.0\% & 221 & 197 & 89.1\% \\
    Gemini 3.1 Pro     & 15.6\% &  3.8\% & 191 & 169 & 88.5\% \\
    GPT-4.1            & 17.8\% &  5.6\% & 207 & 191 & 92.3\% \\
    GPT-4.1 Mini       & 11.8\% &  8.4\% & 147 & 103 & 70.1\% \\
    GPT-4.1 Nano       & 36.3\% & 42.0\% & 452 & 318 & 70.4\% \\
    \midrule
    \textbf{Mean}      & 19.5\% &  6.5\% & 256 & 218 & 82.7\% \\
    \bottomrule
  \end{tabular}
  \caption{M0 classification-gate rejection cascade. Laz.\ = laziness rate (FN
  fraction of positives). Gate-FN = false negatives where
  \texttt{predicted\_no\_clause}\,=\,True. Gate\,\% = Gate-FN\,/\,M0\,FN\,$\times$100.
  Gemini 2.5 Flash and Pro: ${\approx}98\%$ of M0 false negatives are gate-caused.}
  \label{tab:cascade}
\end{table}

The Gemini\,2.5 family is the most severe case: wrong-rejection rates of $40.9\%$
and $31.7\%$ versus $2.7\%$ and $1.5\%$ for B1, with ${\geq}97.8\%$ of M0 false
negatives attributable to the gate. The pattern generalises across all eleven models:
on average $82.7\%$ of M0 false negatives are gate-caused. GPT-4.1\,Nano is the
exception --- its B1 laziness ($42.0\%$) exceeds M0's ($36.3\%$), because the model
is too weak to reliably extract clauses even without the gate.

The cascade is more severe on rare categories: the Gemini\,2.5\,Flash rare-tier
$F_2$ drops from $0.719$ (B1) to $0.381$ (M0), a $0.338$-point loss entirely
attributable to the gate. This interaction explains why M0 widens the rare--common
gap (Section~\ref{sec:res:h2}).

The cost analysis places B1 on the Pareto frontier: zero configurations are both
cheaper and more accurate than B1 for any given model. The 24\%--70\% premium
attached to multi-agent configurations is uncompensated by aggregate $F_2$ gains. In
workflows where the per-agent trace adds operational value (obligation tracking,
audit-trail generation), this premium may be acceptable; the Chapter~6 discussion
elaborates these conditions.
