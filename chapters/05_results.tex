\chapter{Results}\label{chapter:results}

Across 55 (model, configuration) cells and 163{,}644 paired predictions, no multi-agent configuration improves pooled $F_2$ over the strongest single-agent baseline by more than $0.011$ points; the dominant predictor of performance is model capability tier, not architecture.

\section{Run Coverage and Sample Accounting}\label{sec:res:overview}

The 55-cell grid yielded 164{,}941 raw JSONL records. After dropping 1{,}297
records ($0.79\%$) with malformed classification tokens --- concentrated in
\cfgZS\ (B0, $n{=}838$) and \cfgCX\ (M0, $n{=}362$) --- 163{,}644 paired
sample-level predictions remain. The B0 concentration reflects \cfgZS's stricter
span acceptance criterion: the majority of B0 rejections are empty-string completions
rather than structurally malformed JSON. Table~\ref{tab:coverage} reports the analysed count per cell. All
statistical tests use the paired intersection of sample IDs for the
configurations being compared; intersection sizes are reported with each test.
The positive rate across the retained set is $41.5\%$: the sampler retains
all positive (contract, category) pairs and approximately $60\%$ of negatives,
upsampling positives relative to CUAD's natural $\approx\!30\%$ prior in order
to preserve statistical power on rare-tier categories
(\S\ref{sec:meth:sampling}).

The pooled means tell two stories. On $F_2$, \cfgZS\ (B0) is nominally best
at $0.801$ with all five configurations within a $0.011$-point band. On $F_1$,
\cfgCX\ (M0) is best at $0.798$ with a $0.087$-point spread. The divergence is
mechanistic: B0 has the highest recall ($0.854$) and the second-highest
over-extraction rate ($0.320$), while M0 has the highest precision ($0.813$) and
the lowest over-extraction rate ($0.130$). The $\beta{=}2$ weighting of $F_2$
rewards \cfgZS's recall; $F_1$'s equal weighting rewards \cfgCX's calibration.

\paragraph{H1 verdict.}
H1 is rejected in its universal form. Eleven of the 22 per-model contrasts in
Table~\ref{tab:h1-stats} are positive and eleven are negative or zero;
no multi-agent configuration achieves consistent superiority across the
eleven-model panel. Conditional improvement exists in two subfamilies: the
OpenAI family benefits from M0 (GPT-4.1\,Nano: $+0.171$; pooled: $+0.052$),
and lighter Anthropic models benefit from M0 and B1 (Sonnet\,4: $+0.025$;
Sonnet\,4.6: $+0.047$). For the Google family, B0 dominates: M0 regresses by
up to $0.210$ points on Gemini\,2.5\,Flash, and M1 fails to improve over B0 on
all four Google models. Pooled $F_2$ is within a $0.011$-point band; the
model-family interaction is the dominant signal. On $F_1$, M0 leads all
configurations pooled ($0.798$ vs.\ $0.737$ for B0), reversing the $F_2$
ranking and reframing the architectural choice as a metric-dependent decision
(Section~\ref{sec:res:rq-summary}). Cohen's~$g = c/(b{+}c) - 0.5$ on the
McNemar discordant pairs is reported for completeness in the released
artefacts: across the 22 H1 contrasts $g$ ranges from $+0.005$ to $+0.363$
(median $\approx{}0.20$, ``medium'' by Cohen's thresholds). All values are
positive --- discordant pairs favour the multi-agent configuration on raw
binary correctness (TP\,+\,TN) --- but $F_2$'s recall-weighted denominator
flips the ranking on most cells because the precision gains accrue
disproportionately to the negative-instance count. The discordant-pair
direction and $F_2$ direction therefore disagree by construction; both are
faithful summaries of different cost structures.

\section{H3 --- Architecture vs.\ Prompt Content (\cfgMA\ vs.\ \cfgCP)}\label{sec:res:h3}

The \cfgMA\ (M1) router agreed with the deterministic category-to-specialist mapping on
$100.00\%$ of requests for ten of eleven models, and on $99.03\%$ for
GPT-4.1\,Nano (29 mis-routes out of 2{,}999), yielding $99.91\%$ routing
accuracy across the panel. The routed-call distribution across specialists is
uneven --- \texttt{ip\_commercial} 11{,}802 calls, \texttt{temporal\_renewal}
11{,}364, \texttt{risk\_liability} 9{,}068 --- reflecting the per-specialist
category populations (17, 11, 13) and CUAD's prevalence-weighted sampling rather
than a routing defect. Routing is empirically a deterministic lookup: the
router's decisions can be replicated by a static dictionary mapping category
names to specialist labels at zero LLM cost. We therefore present no figure for
routing accuracy and treat M1 vs.\ M2 as a test of whether dispatch architecture
--- with routing held empirically constant --- adds value over a single-agent
prompt containing the same specialist text.

\paragraph{H3 verdict.}
H3 is supported at the per-model level: \cfgMA\ and \cfgCP\ are statistically
distinguishable on $F_2$ for all 11 models after BH correction (McNemar
two-sided, $\alpha{=}0.05$, $m{=}11$). The direction of the difference, however,

hurts precision-oriented models (GPT-family, $\Delta F_2 {=} -0.056$ to

single-agent consolidated prompt produces tighter, more precise extractions
than the routed specialist pipeline. A deterministic lookup replaces the LLM
router without measurable $F_2$ loss.

structure (Section~\ref{sec:auditability}) but also the schema-enforced
grounding that the topology makes operationally feasible.

\section{Contract-Length Moderation}\label{sec:res:length}

The cascade analysis (Section~\ref{sec:res:cascade}) and tier analysis
(Figure~\ref{fig:tier-winners}) treat all contracts uniformly. Contract length
provides an orthogonal moderating variable: the context-window motivation for
multi-agent decomposition predicts that routing clauses to specialists should
help on \emph{longer} contracts, where a monolithic prompt must attend over
more irrelevant text. We test this prediction by splitting all 102 CUAD test contracts into quartiles
by character count and computing per-configuration $F_2$ within each quartile.
The main evaluation already uses all 102 contracts of the CUAD test split, so the
quartile analysis here is a length-stratified view of the headline results in
Sections~\ref{sec:res:h1}--\ref{sec:res:cascade} rather than a separate evaluation
envelope.

Table~\ref{tab:length-quartile} shows the opposite of the predicted pattern.
On Q1 contracts (under 14{,}384 characters), M1 leads by $+3.7$ percentage
points over B0 and M0 by $+2.0$ points. On Q4 contracts (over 62{,}272
characters), B0 leads by $+3.2$ points over M0 and $+4.1$ over M1. The
crossing point lies between Q2 and Q3 (${\approx}25{,}900$ characters). The
mechanism is plausibly gate-induced: on long contracts the M0 classifier is
exposed to larger token windows, increasing the probability of conservative
\texttt{predicted\_no\_clause} decisions that trigger the cascade documented
in Section~\ref{sec:res:cascade}. For M1, long-contract degradation may
reflect specialist prompt dilution when many clause types from one domain
appear in a single long document. In either case, multi-agent decomposition
as implemented offers no context-window advantage over a zero-shot prompt on
CUAD-class contract lengths.

\section{Answers to Research Questions}\label{sec:res:rq-summary}

\paragraph{RQ1 (which architectural pattern wins, and under what conditions).}
No architectural pattern wins universally on $F_2$. Pooled $F_2$ ranks within a
$0.011$-point band, and per-model winners are split four ways across the panel.
The dominant signal is the model-family interaction: the Anthropic family peaks
at B1 ($\overline{F_2} = 0.851$), the Google family at B0 ($0.826$), and the
OpenAI family at M0 ($0.783$). The choice of evaluation metric also flips the
ranking: M0 dominates the panel on $F_1$ ($0.798$ vs.\ $0.737$ for B0) by
suppressing over-extraction. The condition under which a multi-agent pipeline (M0) is preferable on $F_1$
is therefore: the model is OpenAI-family, the practitioner accepts the
$1.67\times$ cost premium, and precision is the primary criterion. Under the
pre-registered primary metric $F_2$, no multi-agent configuration consistently
dominates B0; all other conditions favour simpler baselines.

\paragraph{RQ2 (cost-effectiveness vs.\ single-agent baselines).}
B0 occupies the Pareto frontier at every cost level. Multi-agent configurations
carry mean cost premiums of $1.23\times$ (M1) to $1.67\times$ (M0) without
compensating $F_2$ gains pooled across the panel. The only cost premium that
partially pays back is M0 on the OpenAI family ($+0.052$ $F_2$ over B0), but
even that is dominated in $F_2$/\$ by cheaper B0 deployments on the Google
family. Cost-aware deployment of clause extraction at CUAD-class scale should
default to single-agent zero-shot on a small, fast model unless the deployment
context provides specific reasons to prefer higher precision over recall.

\paragraph{RQ3 (auditable reasoning traces).}
Addressed by system design in Chapter~\ref{chapter:system-design}. The M1
trace structure produces explicit parent-child decision attribution --- routing
decision, specialist extraction, validation outcome --- that B0 does not.
Three tradeoffs attend this auditability surplus. Token storage scales with
trace depth: each M1 call emits a LangFuse trace of approximately $4\times$
the token volume of a B0 call (orchestrator hop plus three specialist
sub-prompts logged at inference time), raising persistent storage requirements
proportionally. Attribution faithfulness is bounded by the trace format: LangFuse
records the \emph{submitted} prompt and \emph{returned} completion but cannot
verify that the model's stated reasoning reflects its actual inference process,
so the trace is auditable but not interpretable in a mechanistic sense. Routing
redundancy is overhead without decision diversity: the M1 router is empirically
a deterministic lookup (Section~\ref{sec:res:h3}, $99.91\%$ accuracy), so the
orchestrator adds latency and cost without altering routing outcomes. Whether the
auditability surplus justifies these costs is a deployment question revisited in
Chapter~\ref{chapter:discussion}.
