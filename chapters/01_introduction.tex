\chapter{Introduction}\label{chapter:introduction}

\section{Motivation}\label{sec:motivation}

Contract Lifecycle Management (CLM) is the workflow through which an organisation authors,
negotiates, executes, monitors, and retires its contractual instruments. The scale of the problem is substantial: organisations forfeit value equivalent to roughly
9\% of annual revenue through poor contracting practices, with contract data dispersed
across an average of 24 systems and 83\% of executives reporting their contracts too rigid
to accommodate change~\cite{worldcc2025benchmark}. Goldman Sachs estimates 44\% of
U.S.\ legal-task time is technically automatable by generative AI --- second-highest
exposure across all professions~\cite{goldmansachs2023ai}.

Frontier large language models (LLMs) match junior lawyers on issue-identification
$F$-scores ($0.87$ vs.\ $0.88$) while reducing review time by
$\approx\!100\times$~\cite{martin2024bettercallgpt}, and independent benchmarks confirm
competitiveness on standardised clause-extraction
tasks~\cite{hendrycks2021cuad,liu2025contracteval}. Single-prompt deployments nonetheless
dominate commercial CLM, and three production failures persist: hallucination, prompt
sensitivity~\cite{chatterjee2024posix}, and laziness --- the silent default to
``no related clause'' even when the clause is present in the
contract~\cite{liu2025contracteval}.

Multi-agent architectures are the proposed remedy. Decomposing clause analysis into
specialised agents promises improved recall through specialisation, improved explainability through
inspectable inter-agent traces, and improved transparency through traceable per-decision
provenance required under Article~13 of the EU~AI~Act~\cite{euaiact2024} and Article~22
of the GDPR~\cite{gdpr2016}. Frameworks including AutoGen~\cite{wu2023autogen},
MetaGPT~\cite{hong2024metagpt}, and Mixture-of-Agents~\cite{wang2024moa} have made such
architectures straightforward to instantiate. Whether they actually improve contract analysis is,
however, an open empirical question.

\section{Research Gap}\label{sec:gap}

Existing legal multi-agent systems --- ChatLaw~\cite{cui2024chatlaw},
PAKTON~\cite{raptopoulos2025pakton}, L-MARS~\cite{wang2025lmars} --- each evaluate a single
architecture on a single benchmark without ablating the architectural choice itself. The
empirical question of which multi-agent pattern is preferable for clause-level contract
analysis under a fixed model and task remains unresolved.

Recent cross-domain work questions whether multi-agent designs confer measurable benefit once
base-model capability is high. Gao et al.\ show diminishing returns for frontier models
whose long-context reasoning absorbs the workload that previously required
decomposition~\cite{gao2025single}. Cemri et al.\ construct a 14-mode failure taxonomy
across seven multi-agent frameworks and report only modest 14--15\% recovery from simple
interventions, suggesting structural rather than prompt-level causes~\cite{cemri2025why}.
Bogavelli et al.\ find no universally optimal architecture across frontier models on
enterprise tasks, with model--architecture interaction dominating absolute architectural
ranking~\cite{bogavelli2025agentarch}.

Prior CUAD evaluations~\cite{liu2025contracteval} have used inconsistent sampling
designs: inflated positive ratios produce optimistic precision estimates and mask
over-extraction as a failure mode (§\ref{sec:lit:cuad}).

No published study simultaneously (i) cross-varies multi-agent architecture and
frontier-model family on CUAD, (ii) samples at the natural class distribution, (iii)
applies paired statistical tests with multiple-comparison correction, and (iv) ablates the
multi-agent design against a consolidated single-agent prompt. This thesis addresses all
four requirements.

\section{Research Questions}\label{sec:rqs}

\noindent\textbf{Main RQ.} Under what conditions, if any, do multi-agent architectures
improve contract analysis accuracy, efficiency, and explainability in enterprise CLM
systems, and which architectural patterns are most effective?

\begin{description}[leftmargin=2.5em, labelwidth=2em, itemsep=2pt]
  \item[\textbf{RQ1.}] Under what model-family and prevalence conditions does each
    architectural pattern --- pipeline decomposition, specialist routing, or consolidated
    prompting --- achieve the highest $F_2$?
  \item[\textbf{RQ2.}] How do multi-agent configurations compare to single-agent baselines
    in $F_2$ and provider cost across clause categories and contract lengths?
  \item[\textbf{RQ3.}] Which architectural decisions produce auditable reasoning traces,
    and what tradeoffs do they introduce?
\end{description}

\section{Hypotheses}\label{sec:hypotheses}

\begin{description}[leftmargin=2.5em, labelwidth=2em, itemsep=2pt]
  \item[\textbf{H1.}] At least one multi-agent configuration outperforms the strongest
    single-agent baseline on $F_2$ (defined in §\ref{sec:lit:eval}; McNemar exact, $\alpha{=}0.05$,
    Benjamini--Hochberg corrected).
  \item[\textbf{H2.}] Multi-agent configurations reduce the rare--common $F_2$ gap
    relative to single-agent baselines (paired bootstrap on gap difference, 95\% CI).
  \item[\textbf{H3.}] The LLM-routed specialist system M1 is statistically distinguishable
    from the consolidated specialist ablation M6 on $F_2$ (two-sided McNemar exact).
\end{description}

RQ3 is addressed by system design rather than statistical hypothesis test; the auditability
properties of the implementation are documented in \S\ref{sec:auditability}.

\section{Contributions}\label{sec:contributions}

\begin{description}[leftmargin=3em, labelwidth=2.5em, itemsep=4pt]
  \item[\textbf{C1}] \textbf{Cross-architecture benchmark.} The first cross-family
    cross-architecture comparison on CUAD: 5 architectural configurations $\times$ 11
    frontier models, yielding 163{,}644 paired sample-level predictions analysed with
    McNemar exact tests, paired bootstrap, and Benjamini--Hochberg correction.
  \item[\textbf{C2}] \textbf{Model-scale gradient.} Architectural ranking is not constant
    across the model panel: larger models achieve their best $F_2$ under simpler
    single-agent baselines, while lighter or weaker models gain from structured
    decomposition. No architecture is universally optimal: pooled $F_2$ across all five
    configurations (B1, B4, M0, M1, M6; defined in Chapter~3) spans a 0.011-point band.
  \item[\textbf{C3}] \textbf{Classification-gate cascade.} A failure mode of
    pipeline-decomposition architectures, named and quantified: the upstream classifier
    propagates silent rejections downstream, suppressing extractor recall.
    Gemini~2.5~Flash's wrong-rejection rate rises from 2.7\% to 40.9\% under the
    pipeline; direction is predictable from a single-agent calibration pass
    (§\ref{sec:res:cascade}).
  \item[\textbf{C4}] \textbf{Routing redundancy with span-quality degradation.} The LLM
    router in the specialist architecture is empirically redundant: it agrees with a
    deterministic category-to-specialist mapping on every model in the panel --- 100\%
    routing accuracy on 10 of 11 models --- and the consolidated single-agent ablation
    matches it on $F_2$ while producing cleaner spans. The routing topology adds cost
    without architectural benefit.
\end{description}

A methodological observation underpins all four contributions. The thesis samples at
CUAD's natural 30\%/70\% positive/negative class ratio rather than the inflated positive
ratios used in some prior work, exposing over-extraction as the dominant zero-shot failure
mode that positive-heavy sampling masks (\S\ref{sec:sampling}).
