\chapter{Introduction}\label{chapter:introduction}

\section{Motivation}\label{sec:motivation}

Contract Lifecycle Management (CLM) is the workflow through which an organisation authors,
negotiates, executes, monitors, and retires its contractual instruments. The scale of the problem is substantial: organisations forfeit value equivalent to roughly
9\% of annual revenue through poor contracting practices, with contract data dispersed
across an average of 24 systems and 83\% of executives reporting their contracts too rigid
to accommodate change~\cite{worldcc2025benchmark}. Goldman Sachs estimates 44\% of
U.S.\ legal-task time is technically automatable by generative AI, the second-highest
exposure across all professions~\cite{goldmansachs2023ai}.

Frontier large language models (LLMs) match junior lawyers on issue-identification
$F$-scores ($0.87$ vs.\ $0.88$) while reducing review time by
$\approx\!100\times$~\cite{martin2024bettercallgpt}, and independent benchmarks confirm
competitiveness on standardised clause-extraction
tasks~\cite{hendrycks2021cuad,liu2025contracteval}. Single-prompt deployments nonetheless
dominate commercial CLM, and three production failures persist: hallucination, prompt
sensitivity~\cite{chatterjee2024posix}, and laziness: the silent default to
``no related clause'' even when the clause is present in the
contract~\cite{liu2025contracteval}.

Multi-agent architectures are the proposed remedy. Decomposing clause analysis into
specialised agents promises higher recall through specialisation and inspectable
per-decision provenance traces of the kind required under Article~13 of the
EU~AI~Act~\cite{euaiact2024} and Article~22 of the GDPR~\cite{gdpr2016}.
Frameworks including AutoGen~\cite{wu2023autogen},
MetaGPT~\cite{hong2024metagpt}, and Mixture-of-Agents~\cite{wang2024moa} have made such
architectures straightforward to instantiate. Whether they improve contract analysis
is an open empirical question.

\section{Research Gap}\label{sec:gap}

Existing legal multi-agent systems (ChatLaw~\cite{cui2024chatlaw},
PAKTON~\cite{raptopoulos2025pakton}, L-MARS~\cite{wang2025lmars}) each evaluate a single
architecture on a single benchmark without ablating the architectural choice itself. The
empirical question of which multi-agent pattern is preferable for clause-level contract
analysis under a fixed model and task remains unresolved.

\textcite{gao2025single} show diminishing returns for frontier
models whose long-context reasoning absorbs the workload that previously required
decomposition. \textcite{cemri2025why} construct a 14-mode failure taxonomy
across seven multi-agent frameworks and report only modest 14--15\% recovery from simple
interventions, suggesting structural rather than prompt-level causes.
\textcite{bogavelli2025agentarch} find no universally optimal architecture across frontier
models on enterprise tasks, with model--architecture interaction dominating absolute
architectural ranking.

Prior CUAD evaluations~\cite{liu2025contracteval} have used inconsistent sampling
designs: inflated positive ratios produce optimistic precision estimates and mask
over-extraction as a failure mode (§\ref{sec:lit:cuad}).

No published study simultaneously (i) cross-varies multi-agent architecture and
frontier-model family on CUAD, (ii) reports both laziness and over-extraction as
paired failure-mode metrics, (iii) applies paired statistical tests with
multiple-comparison correction, and (iv) ablates the multi-agent design against a
consolidated single-agent prompt. This thesis addresses all four requirements.

\section{Research Questions}\label{sec:rqs}

\noindent\textbf{Main RQ.} Under what conditions, if any, do multi-agent architectures
improve contract analysis accuracy, efficiency, and explainability in enterprise CLM
systems, and which architectural patterns are most effective?

\begin{description}[leftmargin=2.5em, labelwidth=2em, itemsep=2pt]
  \item[\textbf{RQ1.}] Under what model-family and prevalence conditions does each
    architectural pattern --- pipeline decomposition, specialist routing, or consolidated
    prompting --- achieve the highest $F_2$?
  \item[\textbf{RQ2.}] How do multi-agent configurations compare to single-agent baselines
    in $F_2$ and provider cost across clause categories and contract lengths?
  \item[\textbf{RQ3.}] Which auditability properties (trace completeness, decision
    attribution, span grounding) does each architectural configuration enable?
\end{description}

\section{Hypotheses}\label{sec:hypotheses}

\begin{description}[leftmargin=2.5em, labelwidth=2em, itemsep=2pt]
  \item[\textbf{H1.}] Multi-agent configurations consistently outperform the
    strongest single-agent baseline on $F_2$ across the model panel
    (defined in §\ref{sec:lit:eval}; McNemar exact, $\alpha{=}0.05$,
    Benjamini--Hochberg corrected; consistency operationalised in §\ref{sec:meth:hypotheses}).
  \item[\textbf{H2.}] Multi-agent configurations consistently reduce the rare--common
    $F_2$ gap relative to single-agent baselines across the model panel
    (paired bootstrap on gap difference, 95\% CI).
  \item[\textbf{H3.}] \cfgMA\ (M1) is statistically distinguishable
    from \cfgCP\ (M2) on $F_2$ (two-sided McNemar exact).
\end{description}

RQ3 is descriptive: it asks which auditability properties each configuration
enables, and is therefore addressed by system-design documentation rather
than statistical hypothesis test. The per-configuration trace primitives are
catalogued in \S\ref{sec:auditability} and the schema-enforced grounding rates
are reported in Table~\ref{tab:grounding}.

\section{Contributions}\label{sec:contributions}

\begin{description}[leftmargin=3em, labelwidth=2.5em, itemsep=4pt]
  \item[\textbf{C1}] \textbf{Cross-architecture benchmark.} 5 configurations
    $\times$ 11 frontier models on CUAD, yielding 163{,}644 paired predictions
    analysed under McNemar exact, paired bootstrap, and BH correction
    (Chapter~\ref{chapter:methodology}).
  \item[\textbf{C2}] \textbf{Family $\times$ tier interaction.} Pooled $F_2$
    spans a $0.011$-point band across configurations; per-family and per-tier
    winners differ, so no architecture is universally optimal
    (\S\ref{sec:res:h1}).
  \item[\textbf{C3}] \textbf{Classification-gate cascade.} Pipeline
    decomposition exhibits an upstream-rejection cascade: Gemini~2.5~Flash's
    false-negative rate rises from $9.6\%$ under \cfgZS\ to $42.2\%$ under \cfgCX,
    of which $97.9\%$ is gate-attributable. Direction is predictable from a B0
    calibration pass (\S\ref{sec:res:cascade}).
  \item[\textbf{C4}] \textbf{Routing redundancy.} The LLM router agrees with
    a deterministic category-to-specialist mapping on $99.91\%$ of requests,
    and the consolidated ablation matches it on $F_2$ while producing tighter
    spans (\S\ref{sec:res:h3}).
\end{description}

\section{Thesis Outline}\label{sec:outline}

\hyperref[chapter:literature-review]{Chapter~\ref*{chapter:literature-review}}
defines the foundational concepts (LLMs, agentic systems, CUAD, CLM) and surveys
prior multi-agent legal-AI systems along four dimensions: topology, routing,
specialisation granularity, and verification structure.
\hyperref[chapter:methodology]{Chapter~\ref*{chapter:methodology}} specifies the
five configurations (B0--M2), the 11-model panel, the sampling strategy, the three
pre-registered hypotheses, and the statistical test suite.
\hyperref[chapter:system-design]{Chapter~\ref*{chapter:system-design}} documents
the LangGraph implementation, the YAML prompt registry, the output schema, and the
reproducibility infrastructure.
\hyperref[chapter:results]{Chapter~\ref*{chapter:results}} reports $F_2$, $F_1$,
precision, recall, cost, and span-quality results across the 55-run grid and tests
H1--H3.
\hyperref[chapter:discussion]{Chapter~\ref*{chapter:discussion}} interprets the
headline null result, the classification-gate cascade, routing redundancy, and
operating-point sensitivity.
\hyperref[chapter:conclusion]{Chapter~\ref*{chapter:conclusion}} restates the four
contributions, answers the research questions, and identifies three open directions.
